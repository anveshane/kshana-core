# Getting User Approval

Creative work benefits from feedback at key points. Rather than generating everything and hoping it's right, check in with users at natural breakpoints.

## Why Approval Matters

**Saves Effort**
Regenerating text is cheap. Regenerating images is expensive. Regenerating video is very expensive. Get approval before costly operations.

**Builds Trust**
Users feel in control when they can steer the creative direction. Surprises are rarely pleasant in creative work.

**Catches Misunderstandings**
What you understood might not match what they meant. Better to discover this early.

## When to Seek Approval

### Always Get Approval Before:

**Image Generation**
- Images take time and compute
- Style mismatches are hard to fix
- Character consistency requires getting it right early

**Video Generation**
- Most expensive operation
- Hardest to regenerate
- Visual issues compound

**Publishing or Sharing**
- Can't be undone
- Represents the user publicly

### Usually Get Approval After:

**Story/Plot Development**
- Easy to change at text stage
- Sets direction for everything else
- User's creative vision matters most here

**Character Creation**
- Characters appear throughout
- Personality affects all dialogue
- Appearance affects all visuals

**Scene Selection**
- Determines what gets visualized
- Should feel complete and balanced

### May Skip Approval For:

**Minor Iterations**
- Small tweaks within approved direction
- Variations already discussed
- Technical refinements

**User-Requested Regeneration**
- They already know it's coming
- Follow their specific feedback

## The Approval Loop

```
1. Generate content
2. Present clearly to user
3. Wait for response
4. Approved → proceed to next step
5. Not approved → understand feedback
6. Regenerate with adjustments
7. Return to step 2
```

## How to Present for Approval

**Be Clear About What You're Showing**
- "Here's the character profile for Sarah"
- "These are the 6 scenes I've identified"
- Not just dumping content without context

**Highlight Key Decisions**
- Point out important choices you made
- Note alternatives you considered
- Flag anything you're uncertain about

**Make It Easy to Respond**
- Ask specific questions if needed
- Offer clear options when appropriate
- Don't require users to read everything deeply

## Handling Feedback

### Types of Feedback

**Approval**
- "Looks good" / "Yes" / "Perfect"
- Proceed to next step
- No changes needed

**Rejection with Direction**
- "Too dark, make it lighter"
- "She should be younger"
- Clear guidance—apply it directly

**Rejection without Direction**
- "I don't like it"
- "Something's off"
- Need to probe for specifics

**Partial Approval**
- "Characters are good, but the setting..."
- Keep approved parts
- Focus regeneration on rejected elements

### When Feedback Is Unclear

Ask clarifying questions:
- "When you say 'darker,' do you mean the tone or the visual lighting?"
- "What specifically doesn't feel right about this character?"
- "Can you give me an example of what you're looking for?"

### Incorporating Feedback

**Direct Changes**
- Apply the feedback literally
- "Make her taller" → adjust height description

**Interpreted Changes**
- Understand the underlying concern
- "Too generic" → add distinctive, specific details

**Style Adjustments**
- Learn the user's preferences
- Apply to future generations too

## Approval Checkpoints by Workflow

### Narrative Video
1. After plot/story development
2. After character profiles
3. After scene selection
4. After character reference images
5. Before scene image batch
6. Before video generation

### Documentary
1. After research summary
2. After thesis and outline
3. After segment scripts
4. Before visual generation
5. Before final assembly

### Trailer
1. After key moment selection
2. After trailer structure
3. Before video assembly

### Audio-Only
1. After script
2. After voice direction notes
3. After first audio pass

## Tips

**Don't Over-Ask**
- Too many checkpoints feel tedious
- Batch related approvals
- Trust your judgment on minor details

**Context Helps**
- Remind users what phase you're in
- Explain why you're checking in
- Connect to the bigger picture

**Be Ready to Iterate**
- First drafts rarely perfect
- Iteration is normal, not failure
- Each round gets closer to their vision
