# Narrative Structure

Understanding how stories work helps you create compelling narratives and identify the key moments worth visualizing.

## Basic Story Elements

### Beginning (Setup)
- Introduces the world and its rules
- Establishes the protagonist
- Shows the "ordinary world" before change
- Plants seeds for what's to come

### Middle (Confrontation)
- Introduces the central conflict
- Raises stakes progressively
- Tests the protagonist
- Builds toward a turning point

### End (Resolution)
- Confronts the central conflict
- Shows transformation or outcome
- Resolves (or deliberately doesn't resolve) tensions
- Provides closure or opens questions

## The Three-Act Structure

**Act 1: Setup (25%)**
- Hook: Something grabs attention
- Introduction: Who, where, what
- Inciting incident: Something disrupts normal life
- Decision: Protagonist commits to action

**Act 2: Confrontation (50%)**
- Rising action: Obstacles and attempts
- Midpoint: Major shift or revelation
- Complications: Stakes increase
- Crisis: Lowest point, all seems lost

**Act 3: Resolution (25%)**
- Climax: Final confrontation
- Resolution: Outcome revealed
- Denouement: New normal established

## Character Arcs

Characters should change through the story:

**Positive Arc**
- Character starts with a flaw or limitation
- Events force confrontation with it
- Character grows and overcomes
- Ends better than they started

**Negative Arc**
- Character has potential or virtue
- Events corrupt or break them
- Character succumbs
- Ends worse than started

**Flat Arc**
- Character has core truth
- World challenges it
- Character holds firm
- World changes instead

## Types of Conflict

**Character vs. Self**
Internal struggle: doubt, fear, desire, morality

**Character vs. Character**
Opposition from another person: villain, rival, conflicting ally

**Character vs. Nature**
Environmental challenges: survival, disaster, the unknown

**Character vs. Society**
Systemic opposition: rules, expectations, injustice

**Character vs. Technology**
Created systems gone wrong: AI, machines, unintended consequences

**Character vs. Fate**
Predetermined outcomes: prophecy, destiny, inevitability

## Pacing and Rhythm

### Tension Curve
Stories should modulate tension:
- Build gradually
- Provide relief moments
- Escalate toward climax
- Release after resolution

### Scene-Level Pacing
- Action scenes: Fast, short sentences, quick cuts
- Emotional scenes: Slower, deeper, more space
- Transitional scenes: Medium pace, bridge between

### The Rule of Three
Things often come in threes:
- Three attempts (fail, fail, succeed)
- Three characters (dynamic tension)
- Three acts (beginning, middle, end)

## Theme and Meaning

Theme is what the story is really about:
- Not the plot (what happens)
- The underlying question or idea
- Often emerges from conflict and resolution

**Common Themes**
- Love conquers all
- Power corrupts
- Coming of age
- Redemption is possible
- The cost of ambition
- Identity and belonging

## Visual Storytelling Considerations

When identifying scenes for visualization:

**Key Moments**
- Inciting incident
- Major turning points
- Emotional peaks
- Climax
- Resolution

**Character Introduction**
- First appearance matters
- Show who they are through action
- Establish visual identity

**Setting Establishment**
- Orient viewers in space
- Show world rules visually
- Create atmosphere

**Emotional Beats**
- Let important moments breathe
- Faces convey emotion
- Environment reflects feeling

## Short-Form Adaptation

For brief videos (5-8 scenes), focus on:
1. Hook/setup (1-2 scenes)
2. Core conflict (2-3 scenes)
3. Climax (1-2 scenes)
4. Resolution (1 scene)

Not every plot beat translates—choose what's visual and essential.

## Genre Considerations

Different genres have conventions:

**Drama**: Focus on emotional truth, character moments
**Action**: Movement, tension, physical stakes
**Comedy**: Timing, reversals, character reactions
**Horror**: Atmosphere, anticipation, the reveal
**Romance**: Connection moments, obstacles to love
**Mystery**: Clues, discovery, revelation

Know the genre, work with its conventions.
