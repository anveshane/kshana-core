# Cinematic Language

Understanding how visual storytelling works helps you create effective images and video. These concepts translate directly into image descriptions and motion directions.

## Shot Types

### By Distance

**Extreme Wide Shot (EWS)**
- Shows vast environment
- Character tiny or absent
- Establishes scale and location
- Use: Opening shots, showing journey's scope

**Wide Shot (WS)**
- Full environment visible
- Character head to toe
- Shows context and geography
- Use: Establishing scenes, action sequences

**Medium Wide Shot (MWS)**
- Character from knees up
- Some environment visible
- Balance of character and context
- Use: Walking, physical action

**Medium Shot (MS)**
- Character from waist up
- Conversational distance
- Most common shot
- Use: Dialogue, general storytelling

**Medium Close-Up (MCU)**
- Chest and head
- Intimate but not intense
- Captures expression and gesture
- Use: Important dialogue, reactions

**Close-Up (CU)**
- Face fills frame
- Maximum emotional impact
- Details visible
- Use: Emotional peaks, key reactions

**Extreme Close-Up (ECU)**
- Single feature (eyes, hands, object)
- Very intense
- Draws attention to detail
- Use: Revealing moments, tension

### By Angle

**Eye Level**
- Neutral, natural
- How we see most things
- Use: Default, normal situations

**Low Angle (looking up)**
- Subject appears powerful, dominant
- Creates imposing feeling
- Use: Authority, threat, heroism

**High Angle (looking down)**
- Subject appears smaller, vulnerable
- Creates diminished feeling
- Use: Weakness, defeat, overview

**Dutch Angle (tilted)**
- Creates unease, tension
- World feels off-balance
- Use: Suspense, psychological distress

**Bird's Eye (directly above)**
- Unusual perspective
- Abstract, removed
- Use: Patterns, choreography, god's view

**Worm's Eye (directly below)**
- Very dramatic
- Extreme power dynamic
- Use: Towering buildings, overwhelming presence

## Camera Movement

### Movement Types

**Pan**
- Camera pivots horizontally
- Reveals environment
- Follows horizontal action
- Use: Surveying a room, following walking

**Tilt**
- Camera pivots vertically
- Reveals height
- Follows vertical action
- Use: Showing tall buildings, someone standing

**Dolly/Track**
- Camera moves through space
- Creates depth
- Following or approaching
- Use: Following characters, approaching subjects

**Zoom**
- Lens changes focal length
- No physical movement
- Can feel artificial
- Use: Quick emphasis, documentary style

**Crane/Jib**
- Camera rises or falls
- Sweeping reveals
- Grand feeling
- Use: Establishing shots, emotional lifts

**Handheld**
- Organic, human movement
- Creates immediacy
- Can feel documentary
- Use: Action, tension, realism

### Movement Purposes

**Reveal**: Show something new
**Follow**: Track subject movement
**Approach**: Move toward subject
**Retreat**: Move away from subject
**Parallel**: Move alongside action

## Composition

### Rule of Thirds
- Divide frame into 9 sections
- Place subjects on lines/intersections
- Creates visual interest
- More dynamic than centering

### Leading Lines
- Lines that guide eye to subject
- Roads, fences, architecture
- Creates depth and direction

### Framing
- Use elements to frame subject
- Doorways, windows, branches
- Focuses attention

### Depth
- Foreground, midground, background
- Creates dimensional image
- More engaging than flat

### Balance
- Visual weight distributed
- Symmetry creates stability
- Asymmetry creates tension

### Negative Space
- Empty areas have meaning
- Isolation, loneliness, freedom
- Contrast with busy frames

## Lighting

### Direction

**Front Light**
- Flat, even
- Reduces shadows
- Can be bland

**Side Light**
- Creates depth and texture
- Dramatic shadows
- Reveals form

**Back Light**
- Creates silhouette or rim
- Dramatic, mysterious
- Separates from background

**Top Light**
- Creates eye shadows
- Can be harsh
- Overhead feeling

**Bottom Light**
- Unnatural, eerie
- Horror convention
- Draws attention

### Quality

**Hard Light**
- Sharp shadows
- High contrast
- Dramatic, harsh

**Soft Light**
- Gradual shadows
- Low contrast
- Gentle, flattering

### Color Temperature

**Warm (orange/yellow)**
- Cozy, intimate
- Sunset, firelight
- Comfortable

**Cool (blue)**
- Clinical, cold
- Moonlight, screens
- Detached

**Neutral**
- Natural daylight
- Documentary feel
- Unremarkable

## Applying to Image Descriptions

Include cinematic language in descriptions:

**Instead of**: "Sarah in her office"

**Use**: "Medium shot of Sarah Chen at her cluttered desk, warm side lighting from the window casting long shadows across stacked case files, shallow depth of field keeping the background soft"

**Instead of**: "The villain appears"

**Use**: "Low angle close-up of Marcus, harsh overhead light creating deep shadows under his eyes, his face filling the frame as he looks down at camera"

## Applying to Motion Descriptions

Describe camera movement for video:

**Static shot**: Camera fixed, only subject moves
**Push in**: Camera slowly moves toward subject
**Pull back**: Camera slowly moves away
**Track alongside**: Camera moves with walking character
**Pan across**: Camera pivots to reveal environment
**Tilt up/down**: Camera pivots vertically
**Rise/fall**: Camera moves up/down through space

Combine with purpose:
"Slow push in on Sarah's face as she realizes the truth"
"Wide shot, camera tracking alongside the running figure"
"Tilt up from the base of the tower to reveal its full height"
