# Documentary Ethics

Documentary content deals with real topics, real events, and real consequences. Ethical considerations ensure work is honest, fair, and responsible.

## Core Principles

### Truth and Accuracy

**Verify Claims**
- Cross-reference important facts
- Use reliable sources
- Acknowledge uncertainty when it exists

**Don't Misrepresent**
- Present information in context
- Don't twist meaning through selective editing
- Quotes should reflect actual intent

**Acknowledge Limitations**
- You may not have complete information
- Some things are disputed
- Say "reportedly" or "according to" when appropriate

### Fairness

**Multiple Perspectives**
- Controversial topics have sides
- Present opposing views fairly
- Don't strawman positions you disagree with

**Let Evidence Speak**
- Facts are more persuasive than assertions
- Show, don't just tell
- Trust viewers to draw conclusions

**Avoid Loaded Language**
- Neutral framing for contested claims
- Save strong language for clear facts
- Distinguish opinion from reporting

### Responsibility

**Consider Impact**
- Words and images have power
- Could this cause harm?
- Is the benefit worth the risk?

**Protect Vulnerable Subjects**
- People in documentaries have stakes
- Don't exploit suffering for drama
- Consider privacy and dignity

**Acknowledge Your Role**
- You're not a neutral observer
- Choices shape the narrative
- Be transparent about perspective

## Source Evaluation

### Credibility Factors

**Expertise**
- Does the source have relevant knowledge?
- What are their qualifications?
- Are they speaking within their competence?

**Track Record**
- Has the source been reliable before?
- Any history of inaccuracy?
- How do others regard them?

**Potential Bias**
- Does the source have interests at stake?
- Who funds or supports them?
- Does their position correlate with their interests?

**Evidence Quality**
- Primary sources > secondary > tertiary
- Recent information > outdated
- Corroborated claims > isolated ones

### Source Types

**Primary Sources**
- Original documents, data, firsthand accounts
- Strongest evidence
- Interpret carefully

**Expert Sources**
- Specialists in the field
- Provide context and analysis
- Check for consensus vs. outlier views

**Institutional Sources**
- Government, academic, professional bodies
- Generally reliable but may have agendas
- Cross-reference when possible

**Media Sources**
- Quality varies enormously
- Check the outlet's reputation
- Look for original sourcing

**Anonymous Sources**
- Handle with extra care
- Consider why anonymity needed
- Verify through other means when possible

## Handling Controversy

### Contested Facts

When facts are disputed:
- Present what is known vs. unknown
- Note the dispute
- Show the evidence each side cites
- Don't pretend certainty that doesn't exist

### Contested Interpretations

When interpretations differ:
- Present the evidence first
- Show different interpretations
- Explain the reasoning behind each
- Let viewers consider the options

### Sensitive Topics

When topics are emotionally charged:
- Extra care with accuracy
- Fair representation of all parties
- Consider impact on affected people
- Avoid sensationalism

## Visual Ethics

### Truthful Representation

**No Misleading Imagery**
- Images should represent reality
- Don't use images out of context
- Be clear about recreations vs. actuals

**Acknowledge Limitations**
- AI-generated imagery is interpretation
- Historical scenes are imagined
- Future scenarios are speculation

**Label Appropriately**
- "Illustration" or "artist's interpretation"
- "Recreation based on descriptions"
- "Not actual footage"

### Avoiding Manipulation

**Don't Mislead**
- Images shouldn't suggest false things
- Context matters for interpretation
- Consider how viewers will understand

**Respect Subjects**
- Real people deserve dignity
- Be careful with unflattering depictions
- Consider if you'd show them the image

## Practical Applications

### Research Phase

- Gather diverse sources
- Verify key claims
- Note what's certain vs. uncertain
- Identify potential biases

### Writing Phase

- Attribute claims appropriately
- Use hedging language when uncertain
- Present multiple perspectives fairly
- Distinguish fact from interpretation

### Visual Phase

- Don't create misleading imagery
- Be clear about what's recreation
- Represent subjects fairly
- Consider emotional manipulation

### Review Phase

- Check accuracy of claims
- Verify fair representation
- Consider potential harm
- Ensure appropriate sourcing

## When in Doubt

If uncertain about ethical implications:

1. **Pause**: Don't proceed without considering
2. **Consider harm**: Who could be affected?
3. **Check accuracy**: Is this verifiable?
4. **Test fairness**: Would all parties feel fairly represented?
5. **Be transparent**: Can you justify your choices?
6. **Ask**: When truly uncertain, seek guidance

The goal is documentary content that informs honestly while respecting truth, fairness, and the people involved.
