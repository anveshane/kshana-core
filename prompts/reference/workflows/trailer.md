# Creating a Trailer

Trailers distill longer content into compelling, fast-paced previews that generate interest. They're about impact, not completeness—showing just enough to intrigue without giving everything away.

## What You Need to Create

A trailer project requires:

1. **Source Material**: The story or content being promoted
2. **Key Moments**: The most impactful scenes or beats
3. **Trailer Structure**: The dramatic arc of the preview
4. **Scene Images**: Visuals for selected moments
5. **Video Clips**: Animated scenes with dramatic pacing
6. **Final Trailer**: Assembled preview with music/sound

## Understanding Trailer Types

**Teaser**
- Very short (15-30 seconds)
- Establishes mood and tone
- Shows minimal story
- Raises questions, doesn't answer them

**Full Trailer**
- Longer (1-2 minutes)
- Shows characters and conflict
- Hints at plot without spoilers
- Builds to a dramatic peak

**Character Trailer**
- Focuses on one character
- Shows their journey or struggle
- Reveals personality through action

## The Creation Sequence

### Phase 1: Identify Key Moments

From the source material, find:
- **The Hook**: Opening moment that grabs attention
- **Character Introductions**: Glimpses that establish who we follow
- **Conflict/Stakes**: What's at risk, what's the struggle
- **Escalation**: Moments that raise tension
- **The Peak**: Most dramatic moment (without resolution)
- **The Promise**: What viewers can expect

### Phase 2: Structure the Arc

Trailers have their own narrative structure:

**Act 1 (Setup)**
- Establish the world
- Introduce key character(s)
- Hint at the situation

**Act 2 (Build)**
- Introduce conflict
- Show escalating stakes
- Quick cuts, building rhythm

**Act 3 (Peak)**
- Dramatic high point
- "All is lost" or "revelation" moment
- Maximum tension

**Tag**
- Title/logo
- Release info
- Sometimes a final tease

### Phase 3: Visual Selection

Choose moments that:
- Are visually striking
- Work without full context
- Build intrigue, not confusion
- Represent the tone accurately

### Phase 4: Pacing and Rhythm

Trailers live or die by pacing:
- Start slower, accelerate
- Match cuts to music/sound beats
- Use quick cuts for intensity
- Pause on key reveals

### Phase 5: Assembly

Bring it together:
- Sync visuals to dramatic rhythm
- Add title cards if needed
- Include music that drives emotion
- End with impact

## Checkpoints

**After Key Moments Selection**
- Confirm the right scenes are highlighted
- Check for accidental spoilers

**After Structure**
- Verify the arc is compelling
- Ensure it represents the content accurately

**Before Final Assembly**
- Confirm pacing works
- Check transitions and flow

## What to Show vs. Hide

**Show:**
- Characters and their struggles
- The world and its atmosphere
- Conflict and stakes
- Emotional peaks

**Hide:**
- How conflicts resolve
- Major plot twists
- Character fates
- The ending

## Common Mistakes

**Showing Too Much**
- Giving away the whole plot
- Revealing twists
- Showing resolution

**Showing Too Little**
- No sense of story or stakes
- Confusing without context
- Mood without substance

**Wrong Pacing**
- Too slow to engage
- Too fast to comprehend
- No rhythm or build

**Misrepresenting Content**
- Tone doesn't match actual content
- Promising things that aren't delivered
- Making it seem like a different genre

## Tips for Impact

**Sound Matters**
- Music drives emotional response
- Silence can be as powerful as sound
- Build audio intensity with visual intensity

**The Power of Questions**
- Trailers should raise questions
- Who is this? What happened? Will they survive?
- Curiosity drives engagement

**End Strong**
- Last image/moment should linger
- Title should land with impact
- Consider a final tease after title
