# Creating a Documentary

Documentaries inform and engage viewers about real topics through research, narrative structure, and compelling visuals. Unlike fiction, documentaries require factual accuracy and source credibility.

## What You Need to Create

A documentary project requires these elements:

1. **Research**: Gathered information on the topic
2. **Thesis/Angle**: The specific perspective or question being explored
3. **Outline**: Structure of segments and topics to cover
4. **Segments**: Individual sections with narration and visuals
5. **Narration Script**: Voice-over text with timing
6. **Supporting Visuals**: Images, diagrams, or footage that illustrate points
7. **Video Clips**: Animated segments
8. **Final Documentary**: Combined narrative with all elements

## Understanding Documentary Types

**Explanatory**
- Teaches viewers about a topic
- Clear structure: introduction, main points, conclusion
- Uses visuals to illustrate concepts

**Investigative**
- Explores a question or controversy
- Presents multiple perspectives
- Builds toward conclusions or revelations

**Profile**
- Focuses on a person, place, or organization
- Combines biography with broader context
- Shows character through stories and details

**Historical**
- Chronicles events across time
- Places events in context
- May use archival-style imagery

## The Creation Sequence

### Phase 1: Research

Before creating content, gather solid information:
- Identify key facts and figures
- Note different perspectives on the topic
- Find compelling stories or examples
- Verify source credibility

Be thorough but focused. You're not writing an encyclopedia—you're crafting a narrative.

### Phase 2: Thesis and Outline

Define what you're exploring:
- What question does this documentary answer?
- What will viewers learn or feel?
- What's the narrative arc?

Create an outline with segments:
- Introduction: Hook the viewer, introduce the topic
- Body segments: Each explores one aspect or builds one argument
- Conclusion: Synthesis, takeaway, or call to action

### Phase 3: Segment Development

For each segment:
- Write narration that's engaging, not dry
- Identify visuals that illustrate the points
- Consider pacing—mix information density with breathing room

### Phase 4: Visual Creation

Documentary visuals serve specific purposes:
- **Concept illustrations**: Diagrams, charts, abstract representations
- **Scene recreations**: Visual interpretations of events or places
- **Supporting imagery**: B-roll style visuals that maintain interest

Unlike narrative video, documentary visuals often represent real things—take care with accuracy.

### Phase 5: Assembly

Combine elements into the final documentary:
- Sync narration with visuals
- Add transitions between segments
- Include any text overlays or captions

## Checkpoints

Get user approval at key stages:

**After Research Summary**
- Confirm the facts and sources are appropriate
- Adjust scope if needed

**After Outline**
- Ensure the structure serves the topic
- Verify segment breakdown makes sense

**Before Visual Generation**
- Confirm the visual approach
- Discuss any sensitive imagery needs

## Source Credibility

Documentary work demands attention to facts:

**Verify Claims**
- Cross-reference important facts
- Note when something is disputed or uncertain
- Distinguish between facts and interpretations

**Attribute Appropriately**
- Be clear about where information comes from
- Don't present speculation as fact
- Acknowledge limitations of knowledge

**Handle Controversy Fairly**
- Present multiple perspectives on contested topics
- Don't misrepresent positions
- Let evidence speak where possible

## Tips for Engaging Documentaries

**Hook Early**
- Start with something compelling: a question, a striking fact, a story
- Don't front-load with dry background

**Show, Don't Just Tell**
- Use visuals to make abstract concepts concrete
- Stories are more memorable than statistics

**Maintain Momentum**
- Vary the pace
- Build toward revelations or insights
- Give viewers processing time after dense sections

**End Meaningfully**
- Don't just stop—conclude
- Leave viewers with something to think about
- Connect back to the opening if appropriate
