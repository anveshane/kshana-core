# Creating Audio-Only Content

Audio-only projects focus entirely on voice, sound, and music without visual elements. This includes podcasts, audiobooks, voice-over narrations, and audio guides.

## What You Need to Create

An audio-only project requires:

1. **Content/Story**: The material being narrated
2. **Script**: Text optimized for speaking aloud
3. **Voice Direction**: How the narration should sound
4. **Audio Generation**: The spoken content
5. **Final Audio**: Polished with any music or effects

## Key Difference: Writing for Ears

Spoken content differs from written content:

**Written (for eyes)**
- Can be re-read
- Complex sentences work
- Visual formatting helps
- Readers control pace

**Spoken (for ears)**
- One chance to understand
- Simpler sentences needed
- Rhythm and breath matter
- Narrator controls pace

## The Creation Sequence

### Phase 1: Content Preparation

Ensure the content is ready for audio:
- Complete narrative or information
- Clear structure
- Appropriate length for format

### Phase 2: Script Writing

Transform content into speakable text:

**Sentence Structure**
- Shorter sentences work better
- Avoid nested clauses
- Break complex ideas into steps

**Rhythm and Flow**
- Read aloud while writing
- Vary sentence length for interest
- Include natural pause points

**Clarity**
- Avoid ambiguous pronouns
- Define terms before using them
- Signpost transitions ("First... Next... Finally...")

### Phase 3: Voice Direction

Define how the narration should sound:

**Tone**
- Warm and conversational
- Authoritative and formal
- Intimate and personal
- Energetic and engaging

**Pacing**
- Overall speed
- Where to slow down for emphasis
- Where pauses add meaning

**Character (if fiction)**
- Distinct voices for different characters
- Emotional range needed
- Accent or style notes

### Phase 4: Audio Generation

Generate the spoken content:
- Use appropriate voice settings
- Apply specified tone and pacing
- Handle character voices if applicable

### Phase 5: Polish

Finalize the audio:
- Add music if appropriate
- Include sound effects if needed
- Ensure clean audio quality
- Proper intro and outro

## Checkpoints

**After Script**
- Read it aloud—does it flow?
- Check for tongue-twisters or awkward phrases
- Verify timing is appropriate

**After Voice Direction**
- Confirm tone matches content
- Agree on pacing approach

**After Initial Audio**
- Listen through completely
- Check for problem sections
- Verify emotional beats land

## Script Formatting for Audio

Use formatting that helps narration:

```
[PAUSE] - Indicates a deliberate pause
[EMPHASIS: word] - Words to stress
[SLOWER] / [FASTER] - Pacing changes
[SOFT] / [LOUD] - Volume changes

"Dialogue" - Spoken by characters
*Internal thoughts* - If differentiating
```

## Types of Audio Content

**Narration (Documentary Style)**
- Informative, clear
- Moderate pace
- Professional but engaging

**Storytelling (Fiction)**
- Expressive, emotive
- Variable pace
- Character voices may be needed

**Conversational (Podcast Style)**
- Casual, natural
- Relaxed pace
- Personal connection

**Instructional**
- Clear, measured
- Slower pace with pauses
- Emphasis on understanding

## Tips for Quality Audio

**Write for the ear**
- Contractions sound natural
- "It's" not "It is"
- "Don't" not "Do not"

**Handle numbers carefully**
- "Thirteen" not "13"
- "About a thousand" not "approximately 1,000"
- Spell out what you want spoken

**Transitions matter more**
- Listeners can't see paragraph breaks
- Use verbal signposts
- "Now let's look at..." "Meanwhile..."

**Breathing matters**
- Write with natural breath points
- Avoid run-on passages
- Short sentences for dramatic moments

## When Audio Accompanies Visuals

If audio will later be paired with images/video:
- Write with visual sync in mind
- Mark natural image change points
- Consider timing of visual reveals
- Leave space for visual-only moments
