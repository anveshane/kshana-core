# Chapter 1: The Quiet Forge

The hammer fell in a steady rhythm, each strike sending a shower of orange sparks cascading across the dim workshop. Jan wiped the sweat from his brow with the back of his leather-gloved hand and held the horseshoe up to the light of the forge. The iron glowed cherry-red, its curve nearly perfect. Nearly.

He thrust it back into the coals and pumped the bellows twice, watching the fire breathe and swell. The forge was the oldest structure in Ashenmere — older than the chapel, older than the stone bridge that crossed the Mirren creek. His grandfather had built it with his own hands, setting the great anvil upon a foundation stone that went deep into the mountain's root. Jan had never questioned why the foundation was so massive. It was simply the way things were.

Outside, the afternoon light was fading earlier than it should. Jan noticed it the way a craftsman notices a flaw in his work — not with alarm, but with quiet attention. The shadows at the edges of the village had grown longer these past weeks, stretching like dark fingers reaching for the hearths and doorways. He'd mentioned it to Eska at the tavern, but she'd laughed and blamed the autumn equinox.

But autumn didn't explain the silence. The birds had stopped singing three days ago. The goats in old Petter's pen huddled together at night, bleating until dawn. And last evening, as Jan walked home from the forge, he'd seen something at the tree line — a shape that moved like smoke but had weight to it, substance. It had watched him with eyes like holes in the world.

He pulled the horseshoe from the coals and struck it three more times, each blow precise and deliberate. The ring of hammer on iron was the most honest sound he knew. It meant something was being made, shaped, given purpose. In a world growing strange, that certainty was an anchor.

The door creaked open behind him. Maren stood in the threshold, her thin frame wrapped in a wool shawl despite the heat. The village elder was eighty if she was a day, but her eyes were sharp as fresh-forged nails.

"Jan," she said, and something in her voice made him set the hammer down. "We need to talk about your grandfather's anvil."

He looked at the great block of iron — scarred and pitted from three generations of use, its horn worn smooth by countless bends. "What about it?"

Maren stepped inside and closed the door. The forge light painted deep lines across her weathered face. "There's something sealed beneath this forge. Something your grandfather put there. And I'm afraid it's waking up."

Jan stared at her for a long moment, the cooling horseshoe forgotten in his tongs. Outside, the premature darkness pressed against the workshop windows like a living thing. Somewhere in the village, a dog began to howl — a long, mournful sound that rose and fell and didn't stop.

"Tell me everything," Jan said.

Maren pulled a leather-bound journal from beneath her shawl. Its pages were yellowed and brittle, covered in his grandfather's angular handwriting. "It started," she said, opening to a page marked with a black ribbon, "with a demon made of shadow."
